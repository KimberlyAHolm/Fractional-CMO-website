PBS Western Reserve

Strategic Leadership

At PBS Western Reserve, strategic marketing leadership involved creating mission-driven initiatives that deepened community connection and elevated the organization's voice. The following initiatives reflect work developed, shaped, and executed in close collaboration with a talented team.

Initiative

Community Impact Report

The station's first Community Impact Report was created to bring greater visibility to PBS Western Reserve's mission, community value, and regional impact. The report synthesizes the organization's work across education, programming, community outreach, and local storytelling into a clear, accessible narrative for donors, partners, government officials, and the public.

Developed through extensive cross-departmental collaboration, the report highlights measurable outcomes, community partnerships, and the station's role as a trusted resource. It also provides a framework for how the organization communicates its impact, helping staff, leadership, and the Board speak with greater clarity and consistency. The report has since become a cornerstone of the station's annual storytelling and engagement strategy.

Framework

Messaging Map

A station-wide messaging map was created to provide clear guidance on how to communicate in ways that consistently reflect the organization's mission. The framework now serves as a foundational tool for aligning internal and external communication and strengthening brand clarity across departments.

Advocacy Campaign

"Public Media Matters"

Public Media Matters was developed as a multi-platform initiative in response to renewed threats to federal and state funding for public media. The campaign provided clear, accessible information about potential funding cuts, answered frequently asked questions, corrected false claims circulating online, and equipped supporters with tools to contact their representatives.

Results

  • More than 16,700 viewers emailed their local representatives and senators
  • Thousands more called directly
  • Campaign drew interest from multiple local legislators
  • Reinforced the station's role as a trusted, transparent resource

Research & Reporting

Community Feedback Survey & Executive Reporting

A comprehensive community feedback survey was designed and executed to better understand audience needs, perceptions, and expectations. Findings were analyzed, tabulated, and presented to leadership, staff, and the Board of Directors, informing programming decisions, engagement strategies, and organizational priorities.

Content Marketing

Hiram College Awareness Campaigns

One of the first goals when joining Hiram College was to elevate awareness. The College had not had an active advertising campaign in more than a decade. Two back-to-back multifaceted campaigns helped make Hiram College appear like it was everywhere.

Campaign visuals and messaging were deliberately different from the typical college campaign — instead of focusing on facilities and programs, Hiram's story was told through its students, focusing on experiences and outcomes with bold, vibrant imagery. Stories were introduced in a full-length recruitment video, edited into 15-second digital spots running on Google, YouTube, and TV in the Cleveland and Youngstown DMAs.

Donor Messaging & Cultivation

Where mission meets generosity.

Donor-facing work requires a fundamentally different mindset than consumer marketing. The goal is not to sell — it is to invite someone into a relationship with a mission they already care about. Getting that distinction right is the difference between a donor who gives once and one who gives for decades.

Philosophy

How I think about donor communication

Many organizations make the mistake of leading with their needs. Effective donor messaging leads with the donor's values. Before a single word is written, the question has to be: what does this person care about, and how does this organization's work connect to that? The answer shapes everything from the channel and the tone, to the story and the ask.

Donors are not customers. They are partners who have chosen to invest in a mission. That distinction changes how you write, how you follow up, how you steward, and how you measure success. A donor who feels seen and valued becomes a donor who upgrades, renews, and refers. A donor who feels solicited becomes a lapsed donor.

When the messaging that attracts a donor is disconnected from the messaging that stewards them, you create confusion and erode trust. The most effective organizations I have worked with treat donor communication as an extension of their brand.

Experience

Donor messaging in practice

At PBS Western Reserve, overseeing marketing alongside individual giving and corporate support gave me a direct view of the full donor relationship, from first awareness through cultivation, solicitation, and stewardship. That experience reinforced that donors respond to clarity, specificity, emotional resonance, and a compelling reason to act.

The "Public Media Matters" advocacy campaign is a strong example. More than 16,700 viewers contacted their representatives by email not because they were asked to give money, but because they were given a clear picture of what was at stake and a simple way to respond. The is an example of donor cultivation in action. People who take action on behalf of an organization are far more likely to become financial supporters of it.

Developing the station's Community Impact Report was another form of donor communication, giving key stakeholders, including current and prospective donors, a clear, compelling answer to the question every funder asks: what difference does my support make? Answering that question well, consistently and across channels, is the foundation of any successful major gifts program.

Approach

What effective donor cultivation looks like

Cultivation is not a campaign. It is an ongoing series of touches designed to deepen the relationship, not extract a transaction. In practice that means content that educates and inspires without always asking, recognition that feels personal rather than formulaic, and reporting back on impact in ways that make the donor feel like a partner in the outcome.

The organizations that do this well treat their donor communications calendar with the same rigor they bring to their program calendar. Every touchpoint is intentional and every story serves a purpose. And when the ask comes, it feels like a natural extension of a relationship that has already been built, not an interruption of one.

What I bring to donor-facing work

  • Understanding of the donor psychology behind giving decisions at every level
  • Experience aligning marketing and development messaging so the donor experience is consistent across touchpoints
  • Ability to translate organizational impact into stories that move people to act
  • Familiarity with the full cultivation arc — from awareness and engagement through solicitation and stewardship
  • A belief that the best donor communication feels like a conversation, not a pitch
