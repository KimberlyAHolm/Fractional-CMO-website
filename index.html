On AI and Marketing Leadership

AI can generate the content. It can't lead the strategy.

A lot of organizations are asking whether they still need senior marketing leadership now that AI tools can produce briefs, campaigns, and content at speed. It's a fair question. AI is genuinely capable, and I use it every day to work smarter and move faster on behalf of my clients.

But there's a gap between output and outcomes. AI can draft a positioning statement. It can't tell you why your last one didn't land, or that the real problem wasn't the message, it was internal misalignment that never made it into the brief. It can generate a campaign. It can't read the room in a board meeting, navigate a stakeholder whose priorities just shifted, or make the call that protects your brand when circumstances change faster than a content calendar can.

That kind of judgment comes from pattern recognition built over time, across organizations, through the specific pressures that mission-driven work creates. Healthcare systems, universities, public media, nonprofits, these aren't environments where you can optimize your way to trust. They require someone who understands the terrain.

What I offer is the strategic leadership that makes AI useful: someone who knows what questions to ask, what the answers mean, and what to do next. Organizations that replace marketing judgment with marketing tools often find they've traded direction for volume. A fractional CMO who knows how to use these tools and how to lead through complexity is a sharper value proposition now than it was five years ago, not a weaker one.

The organizations I work with don't need more content. They need someone who knows what the content is for.

Common Questions

What you may be wondering.

What is a fractional CMO?

A fractional CMO is a senior marketing executive who works with an organization on a part-time or retainer basis, providing the same strategic leadership as a full-time Chief Marketing Officer without the cost of a permanent hire. They are embedded in the leadership team and own marketing outcomes.

How is a fractional CMO different from a marketing consultant?

A marketing consultant typically delivers a specific project or set of recommendations, then steps away. A fractional CMO is an ongoing strategic partner embedded in your leadership team, attending meetings, guiding decisions, managing execution, and accountable for results over time.

What kinds of organizations benefit most from fractional CMO support?

Nonprofits, healthcare organizations, higher education institutions, and mission-driven businesses that need senior marketing leadership but cannot justify the cost of a full-time CMO. Organizations in leadership transition, rebrand, or growth phases also benefit significantly.

How does a fractional CMO engagement typically work?

Engagements begin with a conversation to understand your organization's goals, challenges, and current marketing state. From there, the structure is built around your needs: a retainer for ongoing leadership, a defined project scope, or interim support during a transition. Most retainer engagements involve a set number of hours per week and regular leadership team participation.

What does a fractional CMO cost?

Fractional CMO engagements are priced based on scope, hours, and the complexity of the work. They typically cost significantly less than a full-time CMO salary while providing the same level of strategic expertise. Pricing is discussed during an initial consultation based on your organization's specific needs.

Why would a nonprofit hire a fractional CMO?

Nonprofits often have the most to gain from senior marketing leadership and the least budget to support a full-time hire. A fractional CMO brings donor psychology, community engagement, and mission-driven messaging expertise at a fraction of the cost, helping organizations grow awareness, increase donations, and build lasting community trust.

Does AI replace the need for a fractional CMO?

No. AI tools can generate content and accelerate execution, but they cannot provide strategic judgment, organizational leadership, or the pattern recognition that comes from decades of experience. A fractional CMO who knows how to use AI as a force multiplier, while providing the oversight those tools lack, is a stronger value proposition today than before AI became widespread.
